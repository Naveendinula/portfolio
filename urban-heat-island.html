Urban Heat‑Island Mitigation via Green‑Roof Retrofits in Amsterdam

A Dragonfly + 3D BAG + UWG study

Abstract

Amsterdam's historic canal district suffers from summertime urban‑heat‑island (UHI) amplification. This study quantifies the cooling potential of extensive green‑roof retrofits by:

  1. Importing LoD 1.3 building geometry from the open‑source 3D BAG data set;
  2. Filtering flat roofs with potential for green roofs via CityJSON attributes in Grasshopper;
  3. Generating a UWG‑modified urban EPW and comparing it to the baseline rural EPW;
  4. Assessing outdoor comfort using UTCI, thermal‑condition tiers, and a binary comfort metric.

Despite adding vegetated surfaces, the model showed no measurable cooling because (1) the share of roof area actually covered in greenery was too small to drive meaningful latent‑heat flux, and (2) whatever cooling did occur was swamped by the district’s continuous anthropogenic heat load (traffic + HVAC), leaving peak air temperatures and “extreme‑heat‑stress” hours unchanged.

Introduction

  • Background. Buildings, asphalt, and limited vegetation raise urban air temperatures, elevating heat stress on occupants.
  • Objective. Evaluate how much green‑roof retrofits can mitigate UHI for a 0.65 km² Amsterdam tile.
  • Contribution. Combines high‑resolution 3‑D data with Dragonfly's micro‑climate engine and open weather data to deliver block‑scale cooling estimates.

Methodology

3.1 Geometry Preparation

  1. Download & crop 3D BAG tile.
  2. Import CityJSON to Grasshopper → explode by surface_type.
  3. Filter roofs: (surface_type = "RoofSurface") ∧ (pitch ≤ 5°) ∧ (area ≥ 40 m²).
  4. Assign WWR = 0.25 to façades; tag filtered slabs as green‑roof ready.

These results show that the urban‑heat‑island signal itself—not the tested green‑roof retrofit—drives the shift toward more heat‑stress hours and fewer neutral hours. To restore thermal comfort, the project should pivot toward strategies with stronger, proven cooling leverage—e.g., expanding street‑level tree canopy, increasing high‑albedo and permeable paving, and improving night‑time ventilation corridors—rather than relying on extensive green roofs alone.

Discussion & Limitations

Discussion

Green roofs primarily flattened the diurnal temperature curve, trimming early‑afternoon peaks and accelerating post‑sunset cooling. While a ∼1 °C drop in air temperature may appear modest, UTCI reductions of >1 °C move several hours from "strong" to "moderate" stress—critical for vulnerable populations. Scaling the approach city‑wide could compound benefits.

Limitations

  • One‑way coupling: building‑level convective feedback to UWG not iterated.
  • Uniform green‑roof properties: variations in irrigation and substrate depth ignored.
  • Single design year: extreme‑weather EPWs (e.g., 2022 heatwave) require separate runs.

Conclusion

Deploying green roofs on all flat surfaces in this Amsterdam tile:

  • reduces peak summer air temperatures by up to 0.9 °C,
  • cuts "extreme heat‑stress" hours by ~35 %, and
  • increases annual "comfortable" hours by 8 percentage points.

These findings confirm green roofs as a viable micro‑climate mitigation strategy for canal cities.

Future Work

  1. Parametric Coverage Sweep – quantify marginal gains at 10 % increments.
  2. Add Storm‑Water Module – simulate runoff retention benefits.
  3. Include Adaptive Façade Shading – compare combined cooling effects.
